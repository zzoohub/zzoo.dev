## The Observation

Writers think in scenes and plot arcs — not sentences. Every AI writing tool ignores this. Sentence autocomplete makes the writer do all the structural work. Full auto-generation strips creative control. Neither touches the real bottleneck: turning dozens of interconnected scenes into a consistent multi-episode narrative.

Narrex's insight: if the unit of AI generation is a scene inside a visual structure, and that structure itself assembles the prompt, authors never write prompts. They arrange their story.

## Who This Is For

**Ji-yeon — the aspiring writer**

University student. She's been imagining a regression-fantasy for months. Characters have names, the opening scene is vivid. But between the idea and 40 episodes of finished prose, there's a wall she's never climbed. ChatGPT was fine for 2 chapters — then characters forgot who they were and subplots evaporated.

She doesn't lack imagination. She lacks a system that holds her story together while she builds it.

*"I want my story to exist as something people can read — without years of prose practice."*

**Min-ho — the struggling serializer**

Published 8 episodes on Novelpia. Readers liked the premise but caught inconsistencies — a character's eye color changed, a subplot went missing. He writes 2–3 hours after work and barely manages one episode per week. He knows his story. He can't maintain quality at the pace readers expect.

*"I need the tool to remember everything and generate drafts that fit the established story."*

## The Core Loop

1. **Paste an idea** — rough paragraph, Notion dump, one-liner
2. **Get structure instantly** — config, scene-populated timeline, character map
3. **Arrange visually** — drag scenes, add parallel tracks, edit characters
4. **Generate per scene** — AI assembles full context and drafts prose
5. **Direct and refine** — edit freely, or direct: "add more tension"

Three clicks to first value. The hardest step in novel writing — going from "I have an idea" to "I have a plan" — happens in the first session.

## Product Decisions

### Why scenes, not sentences or chapters

The fundamental question: what unit does the AI generate?

Sentence-level (Sudowrite, TypeTak) makes the writer do all structural work. Full auto-generation (ChatGPT) removes control and loses consistency after a few exchanges. Scene-level is the sweet spot — enough context for coherent output, enough granularity for the author to stay in charge.

The trade-off: Narrex can't polish individual sentences the way Sudowrite can. Acceptable — the bottleneck for aspiring novelists is scene development, not sentence craft.

### Why a video editor, not a word processor

The workspace is modeled after an NLE like Premiere or CapCut. Scenes are clips on a multi-track timeline, not paragraphs in a document.

- Writers naturally think in parallel storylines — multi-track supports that directly
- Scenes have extent (duration), not just position — enabling automatic simultaneity detection across tracks
- The NLE metaphor is intuitive for digital-native users who've edited a video or GarageBand track

The rejected alternative: a traditional chapter-based view. Chapters conflate "what happens" with "how it's packaged for readers." Narrex separates events from episodes — multiple small events can merge into one episode, one large event can span several. Dragging an episode divider on the timeline re-partitions chapters and the AI prompt auto-rebuilds. No other tool makes this distinction.

### Why "director," not "writer"

AI generates drafts. The author directs and refines. This framing shifts the user from "writer who can't write" to "director who shapes output." Direction-based editing ("more tension," "increase dialogue") lowers the craft barrier without removing creative ownership.

The risk: users may over-rely on AI instead of developing their own voice. The key validation signal for Phase 1: do users spend more time editing than generating?

## Progressive Disclosure

The primary users are non-technical hobbyist writers. The interface must not overwhelm on first contact.

First load: single-track timeline, config bar, character map. Multi-track, branch/merge, and keyboard shortcuts stay hidden until the user interacts with the timeline. Direction-based editing appears only after the first draft is generated. Features reveal themselves as the user's needs grow — never before.

## Competitive Landscape

8 tools reviewed — 4 international, 4 Korean. The gap: no tool combines a visual timeline, scene-level AI generation, and Korean web novel specialization.

International tools (Sudowrite, Novelcrafter) are English-only with no visual timeline. Korean tools (AIWC, TypeTak) offer full auto-generation with no quality control, or sentence-level assist with weak output. The scene-level sweet spot inside a visual structure is unoccupied.
