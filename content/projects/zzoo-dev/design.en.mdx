## The audience problem

Developer portfolios pretend they have one reader. They don't. A hiring manager, a fellow developer, and a search crawler visit the same URL and want completely different things.

The hiring manager needs a signal in five seconds: can this person solve my problem? The developer skips straight to architecture decisions and code. The crawler needs structured data it can parse — JSON-LD, clean headings, semantic HTML.

Designing for one audience is a layout problem. Designing for three simultaneously is an information architecture problem. That distinction drove every decision on this site.

## Who visits

**The evaluator — 5 seconds.**

A startup founder or engineering manager, scanning. They land on a project page and decide within five seconds whether to keep reading or leave. They care about outcomes, not implementation. They will not click the Engineering tab.

What they need above the fold: what this person builds, proof they can ship, and one clear path to make contact.

**The peer — the Engineering tab.**

A fellow developer who found the site through a blog post or a project link. They skip Overview entirely. Trade-offs, architecture diagrams, and "why not X" are what they came for.

What they need: honest reasoning about decisions, not a sales pitch.

**The machine — structured data.**

A Google crawler building a search result. Perplexity composing an answer. ChatGPT pulling facts about "developers who specialize in X." None of them read prose — they parse schemas, headings, and metadata.

What they need: JSON-LD entities, semantic HTML, hreflang alternates, and clean content they can extract without guessing.

## Three tabs, not one long page

The obvious design was a single scrolling page per project: problem, solution, tech stack, architecture. Most portfolios do this.

The problem: it forces every reader through every section. An engineer endures business context to reach the architecture. A hiring manager hits a code block and bounces.

The three-tab system lets each audience self-select:

- **Overview** — what is this, why does it exist, what happened. Short, outcome-focused, scannable.
- **Design** — product decisions, user thinking, trade-offs. What was rejected and why.
- **Engineering** — architecture, implementation, technical choices.

The trade-off is discoverability. Some visitors might not notice the Design or Engineering tabs exist. Mitigation: the tab bar is always visible, and Overview naturally references what the other tabs contain.

## The five-second test

Every page answers one question above the fold: can this developer help me?

This principle eliminated a lot of common portfolio patterns. No full-screen hero images. No animated introductions before content loads. No "About Me" text above the project list. The visitor's first five seconds are spent reading, not waiting.

The home page loads directly into a value proposition, an availability indicator, and the project list. No splash screen. No decorative gap between the visitor and the content.

## Search visibility as architecture

SEO is usually added after a site is built. Here, it is a structural constraint that shaped the HTML from day one.

Three discovery channels, simultaneously:

- **Traditional search** — semantic HTML, XML sitemap with hreflang, canonical URLs per locale.
- **Answer engines** — JSON-LD schemas for every entity type: Person, WebSite, BlogPosting, SoftwareApplication, BreadcrumbList.
- **Generative AI** — `llms.txt` at the site root plus clean MDX that LLMs can extract and cite.

The design consequence: every heading must be descriptive (not clever), every page must have a single clear topic, and every piece of metadata must be machine-readable. Creative-but-vague section titles, multi-topic pages, and JavaScript-rendered content that crawlers cannot parse — all ruled out.

## Bilingual UX

Two languages, one button. When on English, the switcher says "KO." When on Korean, it says "EN." A dropdown would add a click for zero benefit.

The deeper decision: English and Korean are not translations of each other. They are parallel content, written natively in each language. Structure is shared. Phrasing is natural to each language. This avoids the stilted feel of translated text.

When Korean is missing, the system shows English content — not a 404. New content ships in one language first. The constraint is "always accessible," not "always bilingual."

## What was deliberately left out

**No contact form.** A form needs a backend. A backend needs a server. A server creates attack surface, maintenance, and spam filtering. A `mailto` link works. For mobile, there is a copy-to-clipboard fallback.

**No animation library.** Every transition is CSS — hover at 150ms, mobile nav at 200ms, floating CTA at 300ms. The performance budget (initial JS under 100KB gzipped, FCP under 1.0s) does not have room for Framer Motion.

**No image carousel.** Project screenshots are static. A carousel adds JavaScript, interaction complexity, and accessibility burden. A single well-chosen image communicates more than six rotating ones.

**No CMS.** Content is MDX in a git repo. A CMS would add a dependency, an account, and a sync layer between the content and the code. For a solo builder, files in git are faster to edit, easier to version, and impossible to lose.

These are not missing features. They are the product of one constraint: zero backend, zero runtime, minimal surface area. The constraint IS the product.

## Why not a template

Portfolio templates — Next.js, Astro, Gatsby — give layout and styling. They do not give content architecture. No template ships with three-tab project documentation, Zod-validated frontmatter, bilingual content with locale fallback, or structured data for AI discoverability.

Notion and WordPress offer convenience but no control over structured output. Neither supports JSON-LD schemas, hreflang alternates, or `llms.txt`. They optimize for writing ease, not for how machines consume the content.

This site chose the harder path: build the architecture, own the output, serve every audience from the same static HTML.
