title: "zzoo.dev — Design"
---

The insight behind this site is simple: a developer's portfolio has two audiences now, and most sites serve only one. Humans visit to evaluate competence. Machines visit to extract structured facts. A portfolio that only works for humans is half-invisible.

That realization drove every design decision — from the information architecture to the content format to what was deliberately left out.

## Who reads this site

**The evaluator.** A startup founder or hiring manager, scanning for signal. They land on a project page, need to know within five seconds whether this developer can solve their problem, and want a clear path to make contact. They will not read the engineering tab. They might not scroll past the fold.

**The peer.** A fellow developer, curious about how something was built. They skip the overview entirely and go straight to the Engineering or Design tab. They care about trade-offs and decisions, not outcomes or CTAs.

**The machine.** A search crawler, an AI answer engine, an LLM building a response about "developers who specialize in X." It needs structured data — JSON-LD, clean headings, semantic HTML — not visual polish.

These three audiences want completely different things from the same URL. The three-tab system exists to serve all of them without forcing any one to wade through content meant for another.

## The five-second test

Every page answers one question above the fold: can this developer solve my problem? The hero section carries the entire weight of first impression — value proposition, a single CTA, and one proof point (availability badge or a concrete outcome). No decorative filler.

This sounds obvious, but it rules out a lot of common portfolio patterns. No full-screen hero images. No animated transitions before content appears. No "About Me" paragraphs above the project list. The visitor's first five seconds are spent reading, not waiting.

## Progressive disclosure as structure

The site map is deliberately compact: four items in the primary nav (Home, Projects, Blog, About). Secondary pages — Now and Contact — live in the footer and contextual CTAs, never competing for header space.

Within each project, the three-tab structure is progressive disclosure made concrete:

- **Overview** — What is this, why does it exist, what were the results. Scannable, short, outcome-focused.
- **Design** — Product thinking: UX decisions, trade-offs, what was rejected and why.
- **Engineering** — Architecture, technical choices, interesting implementation details.

A hiring manager reads one tab. A product designer reads two. A fellow engineer reads all three. Nobody reads content meant for someone else.

## Content format: why three tabs instead of one long page

The alternative was a single scrolling page per project — problem, solution, tech stack, architecture, all in sequence. Most portfolio sites do this.

The problem: it forces every reader through every section. An engineer sits through the business context to reach the architecture diagram. A non-technical evaluator hits a code block and bounces.

Tabs let each audience self-select. The tradeoff is discoverability — some visitors might not realize the Design or Engineering tabs exist. The mitigation: the tab bar is always visible, and the Overview gently references what the other tabs contain.

## Search visibility as a design constraint

SEO is usually an afterthought — something a developer adds after the site is built. Here, it is a structural constraint that shaped the HTML, the content format, and the metadata architecture from day one.

The site targets three discovery channels simultaneously: traditional search (semantic HTML, sitemaps, hreflang), answer engines (JSON-LD schemas for every entity type), and generative AI (an `llms.txt` file plus clean, well-structured MDX that LLMs can extract and cite).

The design consequence: every heading must be meaningful (not clever), every page must have a single clear topic, and every piece of metadata must be machine-readable. This rules out creative-but-vague section titles, multi-topic pages, and JavaScript-rendered content that crawlers cannot parse.

## Bilingual UX: two locales, not a translation layer

With only two languages, the language switcher is a single button — "KO" when on English, "EN" when on Korean. A dropdown would add a click for zero benefit.

The deeper design decision: English and Korean are not translations of each other. They are parallel content, written natively in each language. The structure is the same, but the phrasing is natural to each language. This avoids the stilted quality of translated text.

When a Korean translation is missing, the system falls back to English content rather than showing a 404. This means new content can ship in one language first — the constraint is "always available," not "always bilingual."

## What was left out

**No contact form.** A form needs a backend, which means a server, which means attack surface, maintenance, and spam. A `mailto` link does the job. For mobile users, there is a copy-to-clipboard fallback.

**No animation library.** Every transition is CSS-only — 150ms for hovers, 200ms for mobile nav, 300ms for the floating CTA. The performance budget (initial JS under 100KB gzipped, FCP under 1.0 second) does not leave room for Framer Motion or similar.

**No image carousel.** Project images are static, intentionally. A carousel adds JavaScript, interaction complexity, and accessibility burden for minimal information gain. A single well-chosen hero image communicates more than six auto-rotating ones.

These omissions are not limitations. They are the product of a constraint that IS the product: zero backend, zero runtime, minimal attack surface — and still rich, bilingual, and content-driven.
