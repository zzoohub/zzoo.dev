## The Willpower Paradox

At 9am, you block Twitter. Disciplined, focused, productive. At 2pm, the same you -- tired, bored, stuck on a problem -- types the password, clicks "disable for 5 minutes," and the afternoon vanishes.

Every site blocker on the market assumes willpower at the moment of temptation. Passwords, timers, cooldowns -- they all give you a door. And a door, no matter how heavy, will eventually be opened by the person who really wants through.

The insight behind Permanent Site Blocker: **irreversibility is not a missing feature. It is the product.**

## The Self-Aware Procrastinator

The target user isn't someone who lacks self-awareness. It's someone who has too much of it.

Meet Minsoo. He's a 30-something remote developer who loses 30+ minutes daily to YouTube Shorts during work hours. He knows it's a problem. He's tried solutions. BlockSite lasted three days before he typed his unlock password mid-afternoon. He edited his hosts file once, but he only wanted to block Shorts -- not all of YouTube. StayFocusd's timer gave him permission to "earn" distraction time, which made things worse.

Minsoo doesn't need more features. He needs fewer options. His ideal tool is one he can't negotiate with.

Key traits:
- Works in unsupervised environments (home office, coworking spaces)
- Has specific distraction patterns -- not all browsing is the problem
- Has tried and failed with willpower-based approaches
- Actively wants to remove his own agency over the unblock decision

## Why No Unblock Button

This is the decision that defines the product.

Every competitor offers some form of "disable blocking" -- password, timer, cooldown, or simple toggle. Each mechanism exists because it seems reasonable: what if you blocked the wrong site? What if you need access for work?

Valid concerns. But they share a fatal assumption: that the user at the moment of unblocking is making a rational decision. They're not. They're making an impulsive one.

The alternatives we rejected:
- **Password-protected unblock** -- Users set simple passwords or store them in the browser. The friction is cosmetic.
- **24-hour cooldown** -- Still an escape hatch. The impulsive brain is patient enough to wait, or switches to another browser.
- **Time-based scheduling** -- "Block Twitter 9-5" sounds reasonable until you realize it reframes distraction as something earned after hours.

The only way to remove a block is to reinstall the extension, which wipes the entire blocklist. That transforms blocking from a suggestion into a commitment.

## Precision Over Bluntness

Most blockers work at the domain level: block youtube.com, lose all of YouTube. But real distraction is more specific. YouTube tutorials are productive. Shorts is a time sink. Reddit's programming subreddits are useful. The front page is a rabbit hole.

Three levels of precision solve this:
- **Domain** -- `twitter.com` blocks everything, including `www.` variants
- **Path-specific** -- `youtube.com/shorts` blocks only the Shorts feed
- **Regex** -- `youtube\.com.*[가-힣]` blocks Korean-language YouTube content

This precision is what makes irreversibility tolerable. You're not making a dramatic all-or-nothing choice. You're surgically removing the specific patterns that waste your time.

## The Competitive Gap

The site blocker market is crowded, but every product shares one fatal flaw: reversibility.

**BlockSite** has the richest features -- scheduling, focus mode, custom block pages -- but the premium unlock button is always one click away. **Freedom** takes the most aggressive approach with VPN-based blocking, but routes your traffic through their servers at $7/month. **Cold Turkey** has the strongest enforcement, but it's Windows-only, paid, and heavy with years of feature accumulation. **StayFocusd** pioneered the Chrome extension category, but its time-based allowances subtly teach users that distraction is a reward.

They're all willpower tools disguised as structural ones. Permanent Site Blocker takes the opposite position: no unblock, no timer, no whitelist, no scheduling. The feature list is deliberately small because every feature is a potential escape hatch.

## Privacy as Design Constraint

Zero analytics. Zero telemetry. Zero external servers. Zero npm dependencies. The extension doesn't know how many sites you've blocked or whether you're still using it.

This isn't just a privacy stance -- it's a constraint that simplifies everything. No server means no accounts, no login, no data breaches, no GDPR obligations. Cross-device sync happens through Chrome's own storage, which the user already trusts. The entire codebase is ~300 lines of vanilla JavaScript with no build step. Any user can read the source and verify exactly what runs in their browser. For a tool that asks users to make irreversible decisions, auditability matters.

## The Blocked Page

When you visit a blocked site, the experience matters. A harsh "ACCESS DENIED" would feel punitive. Instead, the page shows a gradient background, the site's favicon, the blocked URL, and a simple message: "Stay focused and productive!"

Motivational, not confrontational. You already made the decision. The page just reminds you why.

## Deliberate Non-Features

- **No unblock** -- The core commitment
- **No timer** -- Always-on blocking is the value proposition
- **No whitelist** -- Exceptions are the beginning of bypass
- **No scheduling** -- "Block during work hours" implies distraction is fine after hours
- **No usage analytics** -- Contradicts privacy-first design
- **No categories** -- Organizational complexity isn't the problem worth solving

Each omission was a conscious choice. The product stays small because small is the point.
