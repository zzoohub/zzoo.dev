title: "Permanent Site Blocker — Design"
---

## The Insight

Every site blocker asks the same question: "How long do you want to block this?" The question itself is the problem. It frames blocking as temporary — a pause you'll eventually lift. And a pause always ends.

The moment you want to unblock a site, you're bored, tired, or stuck. That's the worst possible state for a rational decision. Every escape hatch — passwords, timers, cooldowns — assumes a future you who's more disciplined than the present one. That assumption fails every time.

Permanent Site Blocker starts from the opposite premise: **the absence of a choice is the only mechanism that works.**

## Who This Is For

**Minsu — the self-aware procrastinator**

26, remote developer. Loses 30+ minutes daily to YouTube Shorts during work hours. He knows it. He's tried BlockSite — lasted three days before he typed the password mid-afternoon. Edited his hosts file once, but he wanted to block Shorts, not all of YouTube. StayFocusd's timer gave him a countdown to distraction, which made things worse.

He doesn't need more features. He needs fewer options. The ideal tool is one he can't negotiate with.

**Yuna — the surgical blocker**

31, freelance writer. YouTube is a research tool. Shorts is a trap — 45 minutes gone before she notices. She doesn't want to block YouTube. She wants to block `/shorts` and leave everything else alone.

Every blocker she's tried is all-or-nothing: the entire domain or nothing. She needs path-level precision.

## Product Decisions

### Irreversibility as the product

The missing unblock button isn't a limitation. It's the product.

We considered a password-protected unblock. But you set the password yourself. You know it. Cold Turkey users report writing it on sticky notes. The friction is cosmetic.

We considered a 24-hour cooldown. Still an escape hatch. The procrastinating brain is patient enough to wait 24 hours — or just switches to another browser.

We considered letting a friend hold the password. That turns a solo productivity tool into a social dependency. Most people won't ask someone else to manage their browser extension.

The design that works: make the cost of unblocking catastrophic. Reinstalling the extension wipes the entire blocklist. You lose every entry and start over. That transforms blocking from a suggestion into a commitment.

### Path-level precision

Most blockers are domain-level. Block `youtube.com` and you lose tutorials, music, subscriptions — everything. That's like unplugging the TV because one channel is bad.

Three levels of targeting solve this:

- **Domain** — `twitter.com` blocks everything
- **Path** — `youtube.com/shorts` blocks only Shorts
- **Regex** — `youtube\.com.*[가-힣]` blocks Korean-language content

This precision is what makes irreversibility tolerable. You're not making a dramatic all-or-nothing commitment. You're surgically removing the exact patterns that waste your time.

### No remove button

Version 1.3 removed the ability to delete individual entries from the blocklist. This was the hardest product decision.

If you can remove entries one by one, the extension becomes "permanent until you change your mind." That's every other blocker. The blocklist should be a one-way door — add freely, but the only way to remove anything is to lose everything.

The trade-off: typos are permanent too. Block `gogle.com` by accident and it stays. But one useless entry costs nothing. Easy removal costs the entire blocking model.

### Zero infrastructure

No server. No account. No analytics. No npm dependencies. Not even a build step.

This isn't laziness. A site blocker has the `tabs` permission — it sees every URL you visit. Users must trust it completely. The strongest trust signal: ~300 lines of readable vanilla JavaScript, no network calls, no third parties. Any user can audit the source in 10 minutes.

## Competitive Positioning

The market splits into flexible blockers that trust willpower (BlockSite, StayFocusd, LeechBlock) and rigid blockers that add complexity (Cold Turkey, Freedom).

Permanent Site Blocker occupies a third position: rigid blocking with zero complexity. No accounts, no scheduling, no categories, no timers. The rigidity comes from the simplest mechanism possible — the absence of undo.

The bet: users who need permanent blocking are done experimenting with willpower tools. They want the nuclear option, and they want it to be free, open-source, and trustworthy.
