## Core Insight

Meal tracking has a retention problem, not a feature problem. MyFitnessPal has 300,000 foods in its database. Lose It! has barcode scanning. Neither solves the real issue: people quit logging within a week because every entry demands 30-45 seconds of active input.

The hypothesis behind Mealio is simple: **if logging a meal takes less than 1 second, people will actually stick with it.**

That single constraint -- photo in, entry out, everything else optional -- drove every product decision that followed.

## Who This Is For

**Jisoo -- The Health-Conscious Improver**

28 years old, wants to eat better but has abandoned three different tracking apps. She liked the idea of food logging but hated the process. Searching "bibimbap" in a database built for American diets returned nothing useful. She ended up typing "lunch" with no photo and no details, then stopped entirely by Thursday.

She doesn't need calorie precision. She needs a visual record of what she ate that takes zero effort to create.

*"I just want to see what I've been eating this week. Why does that require a 5-minute data entry session?"*

**Minho -- The Nutrition Tracker**

32, actively managing macros for a gym routine. He already tracks protein and calories, but the repetitive manual input burns him out. He's tried automating it with barcode scanners, but most of what he eats is home-cooked Korean food with no barcode.

He wants accurate-enough nutrition data without typing numbers for every meal.

*"If I could just take a photo and get a rough macro estimate, I'd actually log every meal instead of just the ones I feel like tracking."*

## The Core Loop

Every design decision flows from one user journey:

1. **Capture** -- Open the app, camera is ready, take a photo. Entry exists.
2. **Review** -- Scroll through your photo diary. See what you ate this week at a glance.
3. **Track** -- Optionally add nutrition, ingredients, notes. AI fills in what you skip.

The critical insight: steps 2 and 3 only happen if step 1 is effortless. The entire product bets on making capture feel like nothing.

## Key Product Decisions

### Camera-First UX

The home screen is a camera. Not a feed, not a dashboard -- a camera. The capture button sits at the thumb's natural resting position in single-hand grip. One tap opens the viewfinder. One shutter press saves the entry.

Why this matters: every other meal tracker puts "Add Entry" behind a navigation step. That extra tap is the difference between logging and not logging when you're standing in a cafeteria line.

After capture, the app writes the photo to local storage, assigns a timestamp, and auto-detects the meal type based on time of day (breakfast before 10am, lunch 11am-2pm, dinner 5pm-9pm, snack otherwise). The entry exists before the user consciously decides to create one.

### Progressive Detail

A photo alone is a complete record. Everything else is enrichment:

- **Meal type** -- auto-detected, tap to change
- **Notes** -- free text, no structure required
- **Nutrition** -- manual input or AI-estimated
- **Ingredients** -- fuzzy search from a master list
- **Location** -- GPS-based, optional
- **Rating** -- 0-5 stars plus "would eat again" toggle

This layered approach serves both personas. Jisoo logs photos and never touches nutrition. Minho adds macros to every entry. Both use the same app without either feeling like they're misusing it.

### Guest Mode with Seamless Migration

Signup friction is the biggest killer of new user activation. Mealio's answer: skip it entirely.

Guest mode gives you 10 entries stored locally on your device. No email, no password, no OAuth prompts. Open the app, start logging. The 10-entry cap is deliberately chosen -- enough to form a habit (about 3 days of meals), scarce enough to create natural demand for the full experience.

When a user decides to sign up, migration is invisible. Every guest entry moves to the server in a single batch. No data loss, no re-entry, no "your trial data will be deleted" warning. The transition from guest to authenticated feels like the app just got an upgrade, not like you switched products.

This dual-mode architecture is the hardest product decision to maintain -- it means every feature works in two parallel data paths. But the conversion funnel data justifies it: users who experience the product before signing up are fundamentally different from users who hit a login wall on first launch.

### AI as a Delight Layer

AI nutrition analysis is deliberately not part of the capture flow. It triggers after the fact, running in the background while the user moves on. This is a conscious product decision, not a technical limitation.

Why not inline? Three reasons:

1. **Latency** -- AI analysis takes 5-30 seconds. Blocking capture on that would destroy the 1-second promise.
2. **Accuracy expectations** -- Presenting AI estimates during capture implies precision. Presenting them later, as a "here's what I think" suggestion, sets the right expectation.
3. **Core dependency risk** -- If AI is required to create an entry, an API outage breaks the entire app. By making AI optional, the core experience (photo capture) never degrades.

The AI analyzes your photo, identifies ingredients, estimates calories and macros, and adds a confidence score. It even generates a witty comment in the language matching the cuisine -- Korean food gets a Korean quip. Users can accept the estimates, adjust them, or ignore them entirely.

### Feature-Sliced Design as Product Architecture

The mobile codebase follows Feature-Sliced Design with five strict layers:

```
app/       --> Global providers, initialization
widgets/   --> Composite UI blocks (entry grid, recent entries)
features/  --> User interactions (auth, capture, feed, search, settings)
entities/  --> Business objects (entry, meal, user)
shared/    --> Infrastructure (API clients, storage, i18n, design tokens)
```

This isn't just a code organization choice -- it's a product architecture decision. Each "feature" maps directly to a user capability. `capture-meal` is the camera flow. `entry-feed` is the diary view. `search-entries` is the filter system. When a product decision changes (say, adding a new step to capture), the blast radius is contained to one feature directory.

Import rules flow strictly downward: features can use entities and shared, but never other features. This constraint forces product decisions to be modular. If two features need to coordinate, they do it through entities -- which means the business logic stays in one place.

## Competitive Positioning

| | MyFitnessPal | Lose It! | Mealio |
|---|---|---|---|
| Entry creation | Search 300K+ food database | Barcode scan or text search | Single tap photo capture |
| Time to log | 30-45 seconds | 15-30 seconds | Under 1 second |
| First use | Signup required | Signup required | Guest mode, no signup |
| Primary interface | Search bar | Search bar | Camera |
| Detail requirement | Mandatory fields | Mandatory fields | Everything optional |
| Offline support | Limited | Not supported | Full local storage + sync |
| Language | English-centric | English-centric | Native Korean + English |

Mealio does not compete on nutrition database size or calorie accuracy. It competes on capture speed and retention. The bet is that an imperfect record you actually keep is more valuable than a precise record you abandon after a week.

## UX Principles

**One action per screen.** The home screen does one thing: capture. The diary does one thing: browse. The detail screen does one thing: edit. No multi-purpose views.

**Mobile-first, thumb-first.** Every primary action is reachable with the thumb in single-hand grip. The capture button, meal type selector, and save action all sit in the bottom third of the screen.

**Show, don't count.** The diary is a photo grid, not a calorie chart. Visual patterns (too many takeout boxes, not enough vegetables) emerge naturally without forcing numerical analysis.

**Forgive incompleteness.** No required fields beyond the photo. No "complete your entry" reminders. No progress bars on meal detail. Partial data is valid data.

## Business Model

Mealio follows a freemium model anchored on the guest-to-auth conversion:

- **Guest** -- 10 local entries, full capture experience, no sync
- **Authenticated (free)** -- Unlimited entries, cloud sync, AI analysis, statistics

The free tier is intentionally generous. The product bet is that a large free user base with high retention creates optionality for future monetization (premium AI features, data export, social sharing) without pressuring early users into a paywall that kills growth.
