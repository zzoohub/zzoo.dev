Most people who hear "vibe coding" think it means you describe what you want and AI writes the code. That's true, but it's like saying the printing press lets you make copies of things. Technically correct, and completely missing the point.

The interesting thing about vibe coding isn't code generation. It's what happens when you follow the idea to its logical conclusion. If AI can execute at an expert level in one domain, what happens when you point it at every domain a product needs? Not just writing code, but designing the UX, setting up analytics, writing marketing copy, figuring out pricing, drafting the terms of service. Suddenly one person can run the whole thing. Not by working harder or knowing more, but by directing AI across every function that used to require a specialist.

I've been building this way for a few months now, and the thing that keeps surprising me is how little of it involves code.

I wrote before about [my AI setup](/en/blog/my-claude-code-setup) -- skills, agents, MCP servers. That post was about the build pipeline. But building is maybe a third of shipping a product. The rest is everything around the code: validating whether anyone wants the thing, designing the experience, launching it, measuring whether it's working, and deciding what to do next. More than half the work in a product's lifecycle produces zero lines of code. And that's exactly where vibe coding gets interesting.

Here's what changed my thinking. I was working on a side project and had the code done in a couple of days. Functional, tested, deployed. Then I realized I had no launch plan, no analytics, no pricing strategy, no terms of service. The usual solo developer story: you build the thing and then stare at it, wondering why no one shows up. I'd automated the easy part and left the hard part manual.

So I started asking: what if I treated every phase the same way I treat code? Load the right expertise, give it context, let AI execute while I make the decisions. It turns out this works. Not perfectly, but far better than I expected.

The lifecycle I run now has seven phases. Validate the idea. Design the product. Architect and build it. Deploy. Launch. Measure. Grow. The first thing to notice is that coding only dominates two of those phases. The rest is strategy, design, copywriting, analytics, marketing, legal. All the stuff that makes a solo developer feel like they need to hire five people.

Validation comes first, and it's the most important thing I added. I used to skip straight to building -- write a product brief, start coding. Now I force myself to write one hypothesis and test it before I write a single spec. The test is simple: build the minimum thing in hours, put it in front of real people, and look for organic pull. Do people come back without being asked? Do they tell others? If I can't find that signal after two or three pivots, I kill the project. This sounds obvious, but I wasted months building things nobody wanted before I made it a hard rule.

The product and design phase is where the director metaphor starts to make sense. I don't design UX. I describe the problem and the user, and a UX design agent produces user flows and screen specs. I don't write marketing copy. A copywriting agent generates variations with A/B test suggestions. I don't mock up interfaces. An MCP server connected to a design tool generates visual mockups while the UX agent works on flows. These run in parallel. My job is to look at the outputs and make judgment calls. This screen is too cluttered. That flow has too many steps. The copy for this CTA is too clever -- make it obvious.

This is the part that feels genuinely new. I'm not doing the work of five specialists. I'm reviewing the work of five specialists. The distinction matters because reviewing is a fundamentally different skill than executing. You don't need to be a great copywriter to know when copy is bad. You don't need to be a UX researcher to notice when a flow is confusing. You need taste and judgment, which are precisely the things that are hard to automate.

Architecture and build work the same way they always did, with one important addition: the analytics plan gets designed in parallel with the architecture. Tracking isn't something I bolt on after launch. The question "why are we tracking this event?" gets answered at the same time as "what's the database schema?" This matters because measurement is what makes everything after launch possible. If you don't instrument your product from the start, you're flying blind when you need data most.

Deployment is deployment. But I've redefined what "deployed" means. It's not "the code is running on a server." It's "the measurement infrastructure is working." Events are firing to the analytics platform. Error tracking is connected. The key activation event works end to end. If I can't measure it, it's not deployed.

Launch is where things get really interesting, because this is the phase most developers pretend doesn't exist. Positioning. Audience targeting. Channel strategy. Pricing. Email sequences. SEO. Launch materials for Product Hunt, Hacker News, Reddit. Terms of service. Privacy policy. Every one of these used to be either something I'd skip or something I'd spend days fumbling through. Now a marketing agent orchestrates the whole thing, delegating to specialized skills for pricing, email, search visibility, and legal. I review and approve. The whole launch package comes together in hours instead of weeks.

After launch comes measurement, and this is where the kill/keep/scale framework lives. Week one: observe. Respond to feedback. Don't optimize anything. Week two: evaluate against the kill criteria I defined during architecture. After that, weekly re-evaluation until the decision is clear. The priority for fixes is always retention first, then activation, then acquisition. Confirm that existing users stay before you spend energy bringing new ones.

Kill means post-mortem, archive, and move on the same day. Keep means go back to product or build and fix the biggest problem. Scale means proceed to growth. This framework sounds simple, but having it explicit and enforced changed my behavior. Before, I'd keep zombie projects alive for months because I'd invested too much to quit. Now the data makes the call, and I make it fast.

Growth only happens after a scale decision. Don't grow a leaky product. This phase brings conversion optimization experiments, referral loops, churn prevention, payment failure recovery. It's also when I add end-to-end tests for the first time. The product survived -- now the core flows are worth protecting.

The whole system is templatized. I have a scaffold project I copy to start anything new. It contains the workflow definition, the skill mappings, the document structure for both planning docs and business docs. Claude Code reads this on the first session and knows the entire pipeline. Docs and code live in the same repository, so the AI always has current context.

What makes this reproducible isn't any individual tool. It's the combination of three things. Skills load expert knowledge -- not as documentation you might read, but as constraints the AI must follow. Agents run specialized tasks in parallel, like a security reviewer and a test writer working simultaneously. MCP servers connect to external services -- analytics platforms, error trackers, design tools, deployment infrastructure -- so the AI can act on the real world, not just generate text.

The director metaphor is the best one I've found. I set direction. I make judgment calls. I decide what to build, what to kill, what to grow. The AI handles expert-level execution across every domain. This isn't delegation in the management sense, where you worry about whether the work gets done right. It's more like conducting. You don't play the instruments. You shape the performance.

The uncomfortable implication is that vibe coding, taken seriously, isn't a coding technique at all. It's a way of running a company. The "coding" part is almost a historical accident -- it's just the first domain where AI execution got good enough that people noticed. But the same pattern works for marketing, analytics, legal, growth, and everything else a product needs. The hard part was never writing the code. It was doing everything else well enough to matter.

I think we're at the very beginning of understanding what this means. Right now it looks like a productivity hack for developers. Give it a couple of years and it will look like a new kind of company -- one where a single person with good judgment can operate at a scale that used to require a team. Not because they work harder, but because the bottleneck shifted from execution to direction.

The bottleneck is you. Your taste, your judgment, your ability to decide what matters. Everything else is execution, and execution is exactly what AI is getting good at.
