# Examples Index

Paul Graham's most celebrated essays. Read 1-2 before drafting — pick whichever best matches the essay you're writing.

## By Technique

### Opening with a surprising claim
- **good-writing** — "Writing that sounds good is more likely to be right." One surprising premise carries an entire essay. Shows how to take something that seems preposterous and prove it true.
- **the-bus-ticket-theory-of-genius** — "Genius = disinterested obsession with something that matters." Introduces a new concept with a memorable, concrete metaphor (bus ticket collectors), then systematically explores its implications.

### The recursive question→discovery structure
- **the-best-essay** — Meta-essay about essay writing itself. The clearest example of PG's process: start with a question, follow the thread, cut branches that don't pay off, let discoveries change your direction.

### Building arguments from concrete stories
- **do-things-that-don't-scale** — Argument built almost entirely from specific examples (Stripe's "Collison installation", Airbnb going door-to-door, Wufoo's handwritten notes). The silver chain: small concrete point → small concrete point → small concrete point, each clicking into place.

### Connecting two domains
- **hackers-and-painters** — Takes two things that seem unrelated (programming and painting) and finds deep structural similarities. Shows how cross-domain analogies generate fresh insights about both sides.

### Systematic exploration of a provocative question
- **what-you-can't-say** — Starts with "what ideas can't we express?" and methodically builds a framework for finding them (the conformist test, heresy labels, time/space diff, mechanism analysis). Shows how to handle a controversial topic with intellectual honesty.

### Voice and simplicity
- **write-like-you-talk** — The shortest example. A single, simple idea fully explored in under 800 words. Shows that a complete essay doesn't need to be long — just honest and surprising.

### Long-form personal reflection
- **how-to-do-what-you-love** — Personal experience woven with broader observation across a big, fuzzy topic. Shows how to maintain focus and forward momentum through a long, reflective piece. Uses sections (Jobs, Bounds, Sirens, Discipline) as natural resting points.
